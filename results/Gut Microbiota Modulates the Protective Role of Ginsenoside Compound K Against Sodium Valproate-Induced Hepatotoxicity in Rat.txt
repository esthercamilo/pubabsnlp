This study aimed to investigate the potential role of gut microbiota in the hepatotoxicity of sodium valproate (SVP) and the protective effect of ginsenoside compound K (G-CK) administration against SVP-induced hepatotoxicity in rats. Measurements of 16S rRNA showed that SVP supplementation led to a 140.749- and 248.900-fold increase in the relative abundance of <i>Akkermansia muciniphila</i> (<i>A. muciniphila</i>) and <i>Bifidobacterium pseudolongum</i> (<i>B. pseudolongum</i>), respectively (<i>p</i> < 0.05). The increase in <i>A. muciniphila</i> was almost completely reversed by G-CK treatment. The relative abundance of <i>A. muciniphila</i> was strongly positively correlated with aspartate transaminase (AST) and alanine aminotransferase (ALT) levels (<i>r</i> > 0.78, <i>p</i> < 0.05). The PICRUSt analysis showed that G-CK could inhibit the changes of seven pathways caused by SVP, of which four pathways, including the fatty acid biosynthesis, lipid biosynthesis, glycolysis/gluconeogenesis, and pyruvate metabolism, were found to be negatively correlated with AST and ALT levels (<i>r</i> ≥ 0.70, <i>p</i> < 0.01 or < 0.05). In addition, the glycolysis/gluconeogenesis and pyruvate metabolism were negatively correlated with the relative abundance of <i>A. muciniphila</i> (<i>r</i> > 0.65, <i>p</i> < 0.01 or < 0.05). This alteration of the gut microbiota composition that resulted in observed changes to the glycolysis/gluconeogenesis and pyruvate metabolism may be involved in both the hepatotoxicity of SVP and the protective effect of G-CK administration against SVP-induced hepatotoxicity. Our study provides new evidence linking the gut microbiota with SVP-induced hepatotoxicity.
